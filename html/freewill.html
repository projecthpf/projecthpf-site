FREE WILL

We have the free will to choose every action — but not to choose the consequences. Learning to use it wisely is everything.

▶
0:00

Free will is the idea that we all possess the ability to make decisions independent of any circumstance.

The debate on free will revolves around the fundamental question of whether human beings possess the ability to make choices independent of external influences and deterministic factors. This philosophical and scientific discussion delves into the tension between determinism — the idea that all events are predetermined by past events and the laws of nature — and the notion of free will, which suggests that individuals have the capacity to exercise genuine agency over their actions.

Proponents of free will argue that consciousness and self-awareness grant humans the power to deliberate, make decisions, and act on their intentions, even in the face of external constraints. They emphasize the subjective experience of choice and the moral accountability that comes with it. Many religious, ethical, and legal frameworks are built upon the assumption of free will.

On the other hand, skeptics of free will, often drawing from fields like neuroscience and determinism, propose that the human brain's intricate workings and the chain of cause-and-effect events in the universe limit the true extent of individual autonomy. They suggest that our decisions might be the outcome of complex neural processes, past experiences, genetics, and environmental factors, rendering the concept of unencumbered free will questionable.

The debate has profound implications for areas such as ethics, jurisprudence, psychology, and even the concept of personal responsibility. The debate on free will remains ongoing and continues to spark philosophical inquiry and scientific exploration into the nature of human consciousness and decision-making.

Regardless of what side of the debate on free will you are on, you are right. We all have the free will to choose every single action — but we do not have the free will to control every single thought. Without a basic understanding of emotional intelligence and how the human psyche develops, we cannot fully exercise our free will. And while we always have free will to choose our actions, we don't have free will to choose the consequences of our actions. So learning how to use our free will is essential to shaping our life experiences.

Thoughts vs. Actions — Where Free Will Lives

▶
0:00

One of the most important distinctions in understanding free will is the difference between thoughts and actions. We do not choose most of our thoughts. They arise — shaped by our upbringing, our fears, our conditioning, our biology. A thought of anger, jealousy, or craving appears in the mind without our permission. This is not a failure of character. It is the nature of a conditioned human mind.

But what we do with that thought — that is where free will lives.

Between stimulus and response, there is a space. In that space lies our freedom. Viktor Frankl, who survived the Nazi concentration camps, wrote that even when everything is taken from a person, the last human freedom remains: the ability to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances.

This is the profound truth about free will. It is not the absence of conditioning. It is the capacity to act in spite of it — to pause, to reflect, and to choose differently than our automatic patterns would dictate.

Most people live almost entirely on autopilot. Their reactions are reflex, not choice. They respond to the world the way their past has programmed them to. This is not free will — it is conditioning masquerading as choice.

True free will requires:

  • Self-awareness — the ability to observe your own thoughts, emotions, and patterns without being consumed by them
  • Emotional intelligence — the ability to understand and regulate what you feel before it drives your behavior
  • Knowledge of the psyche — understanding why you think and feel the way you do, including the wounds and beliefs formed in childhood
  • Present-moment awareness — the ability to be here, now, instead of living on autopilot from the past

The more self-aware you become, the more genuinely free you are. Free will is not a given — it is something that must be cultivated.

Free Will and the Law of Consequences

▶
0:00

Free will is inseparable from responsibility. You are free to choose your actions — but you are not free to choose their consequences. Consequences flow from choices with the same inevitability that physics governs the physical world. This is not punishment. It is simply how reality works.

This principle is liberating once truly understood. It means that the quality of your life is, to a remarkable degree, a reflection of the choices you have made — and that by changing your choices, you change your outcomes. You are not a passive victim of circumstance. You are an active participant in the creation of your experience.

It also means that blame is a dead end. Blaming others for your circumstances keeps your focus — and your power — outside yourself. Taking ownership of your choices, even when those choices were shaped by painful circumstances, is the beginning of genuine freedom.

Consider the difference between these two orientations:

  • Victim orientation: "This happened to me. I have no power. It's their fault."
  • Ownership orientation: "This happened. What choices do I have now? What will I do with this?"

The victim orientation feels safer in the short term — it protects the ego from accountability. But it trades freedom for comfort. The ownership orientation is harder — it requires honest self-examination and the willingness to be wrong. But it is the only orientation that leads to genuine change.

Free will, then, is not just a philosophical concept. It is a practical tool for shaping your life — one choice at a time, with full awareness of the consequences those choices carry.

How to Exercise Your Free Will

▶
0:00

Free will is a capacity that can be strengthened. Here is how to begin exercising it more fully:

  • Pause before you react. The single most powerful practice for expanding free will is the pause. Before you respond to a situation — especially an emotionally charged one — take one breath. That breath is the space between stimulus and response. It is the doorway to genuine choice.
  • Study your own patterns. Notice where you behave automatically. Where do you always get defensive? Where do you always give up? Where do you consistently self-sabotage? These automatic patterns are where your free will is not yet operating. Awareness of them is the first step to changing them.
  • Develop emotional intelligence. Emotions are information, not commands. Learning to feel your feelings without being controlled by them is one of the highest expressions of free will. Therapy, journaling, meditation, and honest self-reflection are all tools for this.
  • Take ownership of your life. Stop looking for someone to blame and start asking: what choices do I have from here? Ownership is not self-blame — it is the recognition that you have agency, even in difficult circumstances.
  • Align your actions with your values. Free will without direction is just chaos. Know what you stand for. Make choices that reflect those values, especially when it's inconvenient. This is integrity — and it is the fullest expression of human freedom.
  • Invest in your self-knowledge. The more you understand your own psychology — your wounds, your beliefs, your conditioning — the less power those things have over you unconsciously. Read, reflect, seek good counsel. Know thyself.

Every human being is born with the capacity for free will. But exercising it fully — consciously, wisely, and in alignment with one's deepest values — is the work of a lifetime. It is also among the most worthwhile work any of us can undertake.

We ABSOLUTELY have Free-Will — but it's COMPLICATED

▶
0:00

Whether a person believes it to be true or not, our free will starts before our conception. Through the study of near-death experiences, we know that our soul — our consciousness — is separate from our human form; that it existed before we took on human form and it will survive our human death. Like energy, the soul is infinite. There is also substantial evidence that our souls choose the human life we came into and the hardships we will face.

With that said, we come into material life programmable by our environment, with serious physical limitations, a dependency on others for survival, and amnesia of where we came from and the planning we did for our life. We still possess our free will. From birth to death, we are either consciously or unconsciously making decisions — reacting or responding to our internal emotions and the external environment around us.

Whether or not we choose or plan our lives before we come into them, we clearly cannot use our free will to determine our life situations and experiences throughout early childhood. Our programming takes over. The conditions we grow up in, the people we are surrounded by, the lessons we are taught, and the experiences we have — all of these shape the lens through which we perceive and respond to the world.

Our free will begins to truly activate as we gain the awareness and tools to examine, question, and consciously direct our own thoughts, beliefs, and actions. This is why self-knowledge is not optional — it is the foundation of genuine freedom. The more we understand how we were shaped, the more we can consciously choose who we become.
